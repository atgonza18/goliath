The Scioto Ridge constraints call on February 27, 2026 covered the current status of multiple
workstreams including DC electrical installation, PV grading, pile driving, MV trenching,
logistics/access, BOM procurement, and substation secondary power. The overall tone was

week, which threatens to stall momentum across grading, pile driving, and logistics. The team
is already in a Force Majeure Event (FME) situation for December and January and is tracking
weather closely. Ground conditions are also creating access challenges for material delivery
trucks, with matted roads and temporary laydown yards not yet complete. Despite this, the
site team expressed confidence in their sequence and trajectory, noting they have adequate
machines, operators, and a clear block-by-block plan.

connector fittings -- which have long lead times and need to be ordered immediately even
before the full BOM is finalized, and (2) the substation secondary power RFI, which is blocked
by a contractual dispute between the client (RWE) and Ohio Mid utility over owed studies,
with an estimated two-month resolution timeline. Aaron bumped the BOM constraint to HIGH
priority during the call given the procurement urgency.

6. RISKS & FLAGS
============================================================

1. WEATHER -- CRITICAL RISK: Three inches of rain forecast for the week of March 2. This
   threatens ALL workstreams simultaneously: grading, pile driving, logistics/access, and
   MV trenching. The team is already in FME for Dec/Jan weather. Spring season in Ohio will
   bring persistent wet conditions. If next week's weather is as bad as forecast, expect
   significant schedule impact across the board.

2. LOGISTICS BOTTLENECK: The site team cannot reliably deliver materials (piles, cable) to
   the field due to ground conditions and incomplete road/laydown infrastructure. This is
   already affecting pile sequencing (had to change from planned sequence because piles
   can't be delivered to certain blocks). This could cascade into production stoppages.

3. PILE SEQUENCE COMMUNICATION BREAKDOWN: During the pile sequence discussion [20:27-21:44],
   there was clear frustration from a team member about sequence changes not being
   communicated: "It'd be nice if somebody lets me know that these plans are changing when
   they change." This indicates an internal communication gap on the site team that could
   cause coordination problems.

4. DMC CONNECTOR LEAD TIME: Tyler's warning from personal experience that DMC connectors
   are "a pain in the ass to get to a job site" combined with the fact they have NOT been
   ordered yet is concerning. If grounding material procurement drags, it could block
   electrical work downstream.

5. SUBSTATION SECONDARY POWER -- TWO-MONTH BLOCK: This is entirely outside the team's
   control (client/utility contractual issue). A two-month delay on secondary power could
   have significant downstream implications for energization and commissioning schedules.
   The team does not even have full clarity on what is needed to resolve it.

6. STANTEC 5-DAY TURNAROUND vs. GRADING PACE: If grading continues at the current rate
   (a block every 2-3 days) and Stantec needs 5 days per submission, there will be a gap
   where distribution crews run out of approved work. Sahil explicitly flagged that "distro
   will be out of work by next Thursday" if weather holds. This is a real near-term risk.

7. HIGH PILE DRIVE TIMES: Machines are averaging 3:50 to 5+ minutes per pile drive, which
   is described as "rough" and "not good for the machines." This accelerates wear on PD10
   machines and could lead to more machines going down. Two machines are already down with
   harness issues.

8. MANPOWER DEPENDENCY ON DELTA: Sahil mentioned two experienced operators on permit at the
   Delta project, waiting for release. The ramp-up plan to 8-9 machines depends partly on
   getting these experienced operators transferred. If Delta delays their release, Scioto
   Ridge's production ramp slows.

9. FME CHANGE ORDER NEGOTIATION: The team submitted $1.1M but expects to receive ~$600K
   against ~$550K actual cost. This is tight -- if the approved amount comes in lower than
   expected, or if additional FME events occur (likely given forecast), cost exposure grows.
